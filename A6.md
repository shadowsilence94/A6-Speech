# A6: Speech Processing — Teaching Guide

## Opening: Why Speech Is a Different Beast

**What to say:**

> "Every model we've built so far — CNNs, ViTs, GNNs — eventually operates on something discrete: pixels on a fixed grid, tokens from a fixed vocabulary, nodes in a graph. Speech breaks that assumption immediately. Raw audio is a continuous waveform sampled 16,000+ times per second, with no built-in notion of 'where one unit ends and the next begins.' Today's lab is about the consequences of that one fact."

**Pause and ask:**

> "If I give you the text 'hello world,' tokenizing it is trivial — split on spaces, or run BPE. If I give you 2 seconds of audio of someone saying 'hello world,' how would you even begin to cut it into pieces?"
>
> Answer: there's no natural boundary. The same word spoken fast or slow, by different speakers, with different accents, produces wildly different numbers of audio frames for the same text. This is the problem the whole lab is structured around.

## Part 1: Speech Tokenization (Cells 3–13)

### The core contrast

**What to say:**

> "In NLP, text already IS a sequence of discrete symbols — tokenization is just a lookup. In speech, you have to DECIDE where the tokens are, because audio is continuous. That single difference cascades into everything else we'll see today: why CTC exists, why TTS needs a duration model, why acoustic tokenizers are a recent and genuinely different idea."

**The three speech tokenization strategies — walk through each:**

1. **Character/grapheme tokens** (Tacotron-style): tokenize the *text* input as characters. Problem: the same letter sounds different in different words (`c` in `cat` vs `ceiling`).
2. **Phoneme tokens**: convert text to phonemes first (`Hello → HH EH L OW`). Fixes the pronunciation ambiguity, but needs a grapheme-to-phoneme (g2p) converter per language.
3. **Acoustic tokens** (modern, codec-based): compress the *audio itself* into discrete codes via a neural codec (EnCodec, SoundStream). No pronunciation dictionary needed, but sequences get very long — 1 second of audio can become 600 tokens, versus 2 words in NLP.

**Pause and ask:**

> "Approach 3 (acoustic tokens) means a language model like GPT could directly predict audio tokens, the same way it predicts text tokens. What's the catch?"
>
> Answer: sequence length. NLP token sequences are short and dense with meaning; acoustic token sequences are long and have to model both phonetic content and surface acoustic detail (pitch, timbre) all at once.

### The alignment problem — the punchline of Part 1

**What to say:**

> "Even after you pick a tokenization scheme, you still don't know which audio frames correspond to which token. NLP doesn't have this problem because input and output are both token sequences of similar length. Speech input (frames) and output (text) have very different lengths — and that mismatch is exactly what Part 3 (CTC) is built to solve."

The `SpeechTokenizer` class students build from scratch deliberately treats an emotion tag like `[HAPPY]` as a single token — exactly the same trick as `[CLS]` in BERT. This is the bridge into Part 5: a single injected token (discrete here) versus a continuous embedding vector (OpenVoice's tone color) are doing structurally the same job — conditioning everything downstream — just with different representations.

## Part 2: Mel Spectrogram (Cells 14–15)

**What to say:**

> "Every model in the rest of this lab — CTC's encoder, wav2vec2's CNN front-end, Tacotron's decoder — operates on some version of a mel spectrogram, not the raw waveform. It's the shared 'image of sound' that turns a 1D continuous signal into a 2D representation a neural network can actually process efficiently."

**The Tacotron 2 alignment figure is worth pausing on:**

> "The left panel — the near-diagonal attention matrix — is a *trained* model's literal solution to the alignment problem from Part 1. Each row is a decoder timestep, each column an input position. The model learned this monotonic path itself, with no explicit supervision telling it which frame goes with which character."

This is a good moment to preview: CTC (Part 3) and Tacotron's attention solve the same alignment problem with different mechanisms — CTC sums over all valid alignments explicitly; attention learns a soft, implicit alignment.

## Part 3: CTC (Cells 16–22)

### The two ideas, concretely

**What to say:**

> "CTC (Graves et al., 2006) solves the alignment problem with two moves. First: let the model repeat a character across as many consecutive frames as it needs — 'HHHEEELLLLOOO' is a valid way to spell 'HELLO' over 13 frames. Second: introduce a blank token for 'nothing new yet,' which also lets you tell apart a genuinely repeated letter (the double L in HELLO) from one letter just stretched across many frames."

### The collapsing function

**Walk through the figure (`ctc_collapsing.png`) directly:**

> "Merge consecutive duplicates first, then strip all blanks. `HHEELLLLOO` → merge → `HEL_LO` (if there's a blank inserted between the two L's) → strip blanks → `HELLO`. Without that blank between the L's, the collapsing function can't tell 'two L's in a row' from 'one L lasting two frames' — they'd both collapse to a single L."

**Pause and ask:**

> "Why does the blank specifically have to go *between* the two L's, and not anywhere else in the sequence?"
>
> Answer: collapsing only merges *consecutive* duplicate characters. A blank breaks that consecutiveness, so the collapsing function treats the two L's as separate "runs" instead of one continuous run.

### The forward algorithm — why you can't just enumerate

**What to say:**

> "There are exponentially many raw frame-level outputs that all collapse to the correct answer. CTC's loss needs the *sum* of probability over every single one of them — not just the most likely one. Naive enumeration is infeasible. The fix is the same dynamic-programming trick used in HMMs: a forward (and backward) recursion that computes this sum in O(T × label length) instead of exponential time."

The `ctc_forward_graph.png` figure is the DP graph itself — alternating blank/label rows, one column per timestep. Point out: **"a path through this graph IS an alignment. The forward algorithm sums probability mass over every path without ever walking each one individually."**

**Pause and ask:**

> "In the DP graph, why are there exactly two valid final nodes instead of one?"
>
> Answer: the sequence can legally end either on the last real label, or on a trailing blank after it — both collapse to the same correct text, so both are valid endpoints.

### What the synthetic training task is actually testing

**What to say:**

> "We don't download a real ASR corpus here — that's heavy for one lab cell. Instead we synthesize frame sequences where each character is stretched across a random number of noisy frames, which has the *exact same structure* as real speech: unknown, variable-length character durations, embedded in noise. If a CTC-trained BiLSTM can solve this toy task, it's exercising the same mechanism it would need for real audio."

The greedy-decode visualization deliberately reuses the **exact same `ctc_collapse` function** students wrote by hand earlier — this is intentional, to make the connection between the abstract collapsing function and an actual trained model's output concrete.

## Part 4: wav2vec 2.0 (Cells 23–28)

### Why this comes right after CTC

**What to say:**

> "CTC still needed labeled (audio, transcript) pairs — just not frame-level alignment. wav2vec2 asks a more aggressive question: can we learn useful speech representations with *no transcripts at all*? This is the speech-domain version of exactly the same self-supervised philosophy from the SSL lab — SimCLR, DINO, MAE all learned image features without labels. wav2vec2 does it for audio."

### The architecture, walking through the figure

**What to say (pointing at `wav2vec2_arch.png`):**

> "Bottom to top: raw waveform goes through a CNN to produce latent representations Z — about one vector per 20 milliseconds. Each of those is also mapped to a discrete codebook entry Q through a quantization module. Then random spans of Z are masked, fed through a Transformer, and the model has to predict — via a contrastive loss against a handful of distractor codes — which quantized entry belongs in each masked slot."

**Pause and ask:**

> "Where have we seen 'mask part of the input, predict what's missing, learn from your own data' before in this course?"
>
> Answer: MAE in the SSL lab, masking 75% of image patches and reconstructing them. wav2vec2 is the same idea applied to audio frames, with a contrastive objective instead of pixel reconstruction.

### Why we probe instead of pretrain

**What to say:**

> "The original wav2vec2 trained on 960+ hours of LibriSpeech — well outside what one lab session can reproduce. What we *can* do cheaply, and what tells us just as much: load a pretrained checkpoint from Hugging Face, freeze every parameter, and run the exact same linear-probe protocol from the SSL lab. Freeze the encoder, train only a single linear layer with labels, and see how separable the frozen features already are."

This is a deliberate callback: the evaluation methodology is *identical* to A3's SimCLR/DINO/MAE linear probing. The point is for students to recognize that "freeze, probe with a linear layer" is a general-purpose way to evaluate any self-supervised encoder, not something specific to images.

**Pause and ask:**

> "If the linear probe gets, say, 85% accuracy on 4-way word classification, what does that actually tell us — and what does it NOT tell us?"
>
> Answer: it tells us the frozen features are close to linearly separable for this task — strong evidence the pretraining learned something real. It does NOT tell us the features are optimal, or that a more powerful classifier (or fine-tuning the encoder) wouldn't do much better; linear probing is deliberately a weak/cheap test, the same as in the SSL lab.

### Why SpeechCommands instead of LibriSpeech

**What to say:**

> "We use SpeechCommands here, not LibriSpeech — it's small, built directly into `torchaudio`, and each clip is one spoken word. That's exactly what we want for a fast linear-probe demo: short clips, clean discrete labels, no transcript alignment needed."

## Bridge: From Representations to Identity

**What to say before Part 5:**

> "CTC and wav2vec2 were both about *what* is being said — transcribing or representing linguistic content. Part 5 flips the question: instead of content, we care about *who* is speaking. The mechanism — extract an embedding from audio, condition a downstream model on it — will look familiar. The interesting part is what kind of embedding, and what it's disentangled from."

## Part 5: Voice Cloning + Expressive TTS — OpenVoice (Cells 29–36)

### Reframing voice cloning as the same "inject a vector" idea

**What to say:**

> "A speaker embedding is conceptually the same kind of object as the `[HAPPY]` emotion tag from Part 1's tokenizer — a vector injected into the model that conditions everything downstream. The difference is what it conditions: an emotion tag shapes prosody (pitch, rate, energy); a speaker embedding shapes timbre and identity. Same mechanism, different axis of control."

### OpenVoice's key design decision

**What to say:**

> "OpenVoice's whole contribution is one architectural choice: don't let identity and style get tangled together. Tone color (who you sound like — formant structure, pitch range, vocal tract characteristics) is extracted once from a short reference clip. Style (emotion, accent, rhythm) comes from a completely separate base speaker TTS model. A tone color converter re-renders the base model's output in your voice, while explicitly preserving its rhythm and pacing."

**Pause and ask:**

> "Why does this disentanglement matter practically? What does it let you do that a single end-to-end voice cloning model might not?"
>
> Answer: cross-lingual cloning (clone an English voice, speak Spanish) and independent style control (clone a calm voice, render it as excited) — because tone color never has to learn anything about language or emotion, it stays purely acoustic.

### Cross-lingual cloning — the "why" behind the cosine table

**What to say:**

> "The tone color extractor was never trained to care about phonemes or language — only the acoustic properties of the voice itself. That's *why* it transfers across languages: it has nothing language-specific to fail to transfer. Compare this to the character/phoneme tokenizers from Part 1, which are inherently language-specific by construction."

This connects directly back to the comparison table at the end of Part 5 — make sure students see the row "reusable across languages? No (tokenizer) vs Yes (tone color)" as the payoff of the disentanglement argument, not an isolated fact.

## Exercise Walkthrough Tips

**Ex1 (Tokenization):** Mechanical but useful — students should notice the emotion tag always counts as exactly 1 token regardless of its character length, while everything else is one token per character. The `[CLS]`-token comparison in 1c is the conceptual payoff: a single token, injected once, conditions the *entire* output sequence through attention — that's the architectural fact students should articulate, not just "it's like BERT."

**Ex2 (CTC):** Part (b) is the one to watch — students sometimes expect all same-length words to get similar probabilities. They won't, because the per-frame distribution favors specific characters at specific frames; a word whose characters don't line up with where the model's mass actually is will score much lower. Part (d), shortening `frames_per_char` to `(1,2)`, should make accuracy noticeably *worse* — with so few frames per character, the BiLSTM has very little redundancy to "vote" on the right character before the next one starts, and noise dominates.

**Ex3 (wav2vec2 vs raw features):** This is the SSL lab's "does the pretraining actually help" question, transplanted to audio. Expect wav2vec2 to beat raw mel-spectrogram features by a wide margin even with this tiny probe — mel-spectrogram features are unstructured to a linear classifier, whereas wav2vec2's contextual representations already separate words fairly well. If the gap looks smaller than the SSL lab's vision results, that's worth discussing: is it because SpeechCommands is an easier task, or because audio's lower-dimensional surface form makes raw features less hopeless than raw pixels?

**Ex4 (Voice cloning — identity, style, and language):** Part (b) is the identity-vs-style check: students should expect *whispering* to be the style most likely to show some tone color similarity drop — whispering removes voiced excitation (no fundamental frequency), which is exactly the kind of signal a tone color extractor likely relies on most. Uniformly high, flat similarity across all four styles is a clean confirmation of OpenVoice's disentanglement claim; a visible drop on whispering is a legitimate, reportable failure mode, not a mistake. Part (c)/(d) shifts to cross-lingual transfer: the expected finding is that pronunciation quality tracks the *base speaker model's* language fluency, not the reference speaker's. If a student's own accent leaks into the Spanish output, push them toward connecting it back to (b) — accent has both an acoustic component (which tone color may capture, same mechanism as the whispering drop) and a phonemic component (which it shouldn't, by design). Both halves of this exercise are testing the same underlying claim from two different angles: does tone color truly stay constant when something else about the speech changes?

## The Big Picture — Speech Model Evolution

```
2006  CTC (Graves)         "Alignment-free training via dynamic programming.
                            Needs transcripts, not frame-level alignment."
        ↓
2017  Tacotron             "First end-to-end TTS — characters directly to mel spectrogram."
        ↓
2018  Tacotron 2           "+ WaveNet vocoder. Near-human MOS on single-speaker TTS."
        ↓
2020  wav2vec 2.0          "Self-supervised speech representations — zero transcripts
                            needed for pretraining. Mask + quantize + contrastive."
        ↓
2022  EnCodec / RVQ        "Audio as discrete tokens. Bridges speech and LLM-style
                            sequence modeling."
        ↓
2023  OpenVoice            "Disentangle tone color from style. Zero-shot, cross-lingual
                            voice cloning from a single short reference clip."
        ↓
2024  Moshi / GPT-4o       "Full-duplex, omni-modal — audio, text in one Transformer,
                            no separate ASR/TTS modules at all."
```

**Closing question for students:**

> "A startup wants to build a real-time captioning system for a live lecture in a language with almost no labeled speech data, but plenty of unlabeled recordings. Which of today's three model families — CTC, wav2vec2, or OpenVoice — would you reach for first, and why?"
>
> Answer: wav2vec2-style self-supervised pretraining on the unlabeled recordings first, then fine-tune a small CTC head on whatever little labeled data exists. CTC alone needs labeled pairs you don't have; OpenVoice solves a completely different problem (synthesis, not recognition).

**Follow-up:**

> "Now suppose instead the startup wants to dub that same lecture into three other languages, in the lecturer's own voice. Which model family, and what's the one architectural property that makes it possible?"
>
> Answer: OpenVoice (or any tone-color/style-disentangled cloning approach) — specifically because the tone color embedding is purely acoustic and was never trained to depend on phonemes or language, so it transfers to a base speaker model in a different language without modification.
